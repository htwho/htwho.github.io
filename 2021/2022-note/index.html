The Restricted Three-Body Problem Part 1

發表於 2021-10 更新於 2022-10

Introduction

Consider two of the bodies move in a circular orbit with the same angular velocity, and they have a common of mass and a constant distance. Compared with the other two objects, the mass of the third body is negligible and taken as a massless small body (i.e. asteroids or satellite)\(^{[3]}\). Then, the motion of the third body is subject to the gravitational influence of two masses, but the third body can't affect the motion of two masses. This is called the restricted three-body problem.

The general restricted three-body problem is complex and chaotic, even small changes in initial conditions lead to a widely different result. Therefore, the solution must be obtained by numerical analysis. In application, it's used for space missions, such as a spacecraft moving to planets, or from Earth to the moon\(^{[3]}\). The mass of a small spacecraft is affected by planets and satellites of the planet, but the planets don't feel the gravitational force from a small spacecraft apparently\(^{[3]}\).

Let define the unit of mass in this system \[\begin{equation} \label{eq1.1} \mu = G (m_1 + m_2) \end{equation}\]

Then the two mass are \[\begin{equation} \label{eq1.2} \mu_1 = G m_1= \frac{m_1}{m_1+m_2} \end{equation}\] \[\begin{equation} \label{eq1.3} \mu_2 =\bar\mu = G m_2= \frac{m_2}{m_1+m_2} \end{equation}\]

Set of axes \((x, y, z)\) in the inertial coordinate frame referred to the centre of mass between \(\mu_1\) and \(\mu_2\). Therefore, the position of the two masses in the inertial frame are \((x_1, y_1, z_1)\) and \((x_2, y_2, z_2)\) (see Figure ). The unit of length is the constant distance between the two masses. And the inertial frame follows the constant angular velocity with mean motion \(n\).

The equation of motion of the particle at the point \(P\) in the inertial frame are \[\begin{equation} \label{eq1} % the label is used to reference the equation \ddot x' = \mu_1 \cdot \frac{(x_1' - x')}{r_1^{3}} + \mu_2 \cdot \frac{(x_2' - x')}{r_2^{3}} \end{equation}\] \[\begin{equation} \label{eq2} \ddot y' = \mu_1 \cdot \frac{(y_1' - y')}{r_1^{3}} + \mu_2 \cdot \frac{(y_2' - y')}{r_2^{3}} \end{equation}\] \[\begin{equation} \label{eq3} \ddot z' = \mu_1 \cdot \frac{(z_1' - z')}{r_1^{3}} + \mu_2 \cdot \frac{(z_2' - z')}{r_2^{3}} \end{equation}\]

The distance in the inertial frame represents as following, \[\begin{equation} \label{eq4} r_1^2 = (x_1' - x')^2 + (y_1' - y')^2 + (z_1' - z')^2 \end{equation}\] \[\begin{equation} \label{eq5} r_2^2 = (x_2' - x')^2 + (y_2' - y')^2 + (z_2' - z')^2 \end{equation}\]

Now, considering a new rotating coordinate system (see Figure ) has the same origin as the inertial frame, set of axes \((x', y', z')\), the position of two masses are \[\begin{align*} (x_1, y_1, z_1) = (-\mu_2, 0, 0) \\ (x_2, y_2, z_2) = (\mu_1, 0, 0) \end{align*}\]

Therefore, the distances in Equation~ and Equation~ can be rewritten as in term of the rotating coordinate system \[\begin{equation} \label{eq6} \begin{split} r_1^2& = (x_1' - x)^2 + (y_1' - y)^2 + (z_1' - z)^2 \\ &= (-\mu_2 - x)^2 + y^2 + z^2 \\ &= (x + \mu_2)^2 + y^2 + z^2 \end{split} \end{equation}\]

\[\begin{equation} \label{eq7} \begin{split} r_2^2& = (x_2' - x)^2 + (y_2' - y)^2 + (z_2' - z)^2 \\ &= (\mu_1-x)^2 + y^2 + z^2 \\ &= (x - \mu_1 )^2 + y^2 + z^2 \end{split} \end{equation}\]

NOTE:
> \(x, y\), and \(z \Rightarrow\) the inertial (sidereal) coordinate system
> \(x', y'\), and $ z' $ the rotating (synodic) coordinate system

The relation between the inertial and the rotating coordinate system can be expressed as a matrix form. The rotating system is related to the inertial system by the simple rotation.

\[\begin{equation} \label{eq8} \begin{bmatrix} x' \\y' \\z' \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos{nt} & -\sin{nt} & 0 \\ \sin{nt} & \cos{nt} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\y \\z \end{bmatrix} \end{equation}\]

Equation becomes \[\begin{equation} \label{eq8.1} \begin{split} x' = x \cos{nt} - y \sin{nt} \end{split} \end{equation}\] \[\begin{equation} \label{eq8.2} \begin{split} y' = x \sin{nt} + y \cos{nt} \end{split} \end{equation}\] \[\begin{equation} \label{eq8.3} \begin{split} z' = z \end{split} \end{equation}\]

Then, differentiate \(x', y'\), and \(z'\) \[\begin{equation} \label{eq9} \begin{bmatrix} \dot x' \\\dot y' \\\dot z' \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos{nt} & -\sin{nt} & 0 \\ \sin{nt} & \cos{nt} & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x} -ny \\ \dot{y}+nx \\ \dot z \end{bmatrix} \end{equation}\]

Equation becomes \[\begin{equation} \label{eq9.1} \begin{split} \dot x' &= \dot x \cos{nt} + xn (-\sin{nt}) - \dot y \cos{nt} - yn\cos{nt} \\ &= \cos{nt}\cdot (\dot x -ny) - \sin{nt} \cdot (\dot y + nx) \end{split} \end{equation}\]

\[\begin{equation} \label{eq9.2} \begin{split} \dot y' &= \dot x \sin{nt} + xn(-\cos{nt}) + \dot y \cos{nt} + yn(-\sin{nt}) \\ &= \sin{nt}\cdot (\dot x -ny) + \cos{nt}\cdot (\dot y + nx) \end{split} \end{equation}\]
