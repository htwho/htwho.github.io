Tisserand Relation T_J

Consider a comet is not close with initial semi-major \(a\), eccentricity \(e\), inclination \(i\). After encountering Jupiter, the orbital element of the comet becomes semi-major \(a'\), eccentricity \(e'\), inclination \(i'\)

From the Jacobi integral \(C_J\), it remains constant value throughout the encounter with the masses \(^{[3]}\).

\[ C_J = 2V - v^2 \\ = 2(\frac{\mu_1}{r_1}+\frac{\mu_2}{r_2}) + 2n(\dot x' y' - x' \dot y') - ( \dot x'^2 + \dot y'^2 + \dot z'^2 ) \label{1} \] For instance in Sun-Jupiter-comet problem, because the mass of Jupiter is smaller than Sun \((M_j \sim 10^{-3}M_\odot)\), we take \(\mu_1\) and \(r_1\) as the unity. refer oto eq. \(\ref{1}\) \[ GM_\odot = \mu_1 = \mu \]

\[ r_1 = r \]

When the comet is not close to Jupiter, so that \(\frac{\mu_2}{r_2}\) term is always small to neglect the \(\mu_2\) term.

\[\frac{\mu_2}{r_2} \ll \frac{\mu_1}{r_1}=\frac{\mu}{r}\]

Where \(\mu_2 = GM_J\), \(r_2\) is the distance between Jupiter and the comet.

Then, we can consider the approximation of two-body problem between Sun and comet. From the orbital energy of Sun-comet two-body problem.

\[ E = \frac{1}{2}(\ddot x^2 +\ddot y^2 +\ddot z^2 ) - \frac{G(M_\odot+ m_{comet})}{r_1} = -\frac{G(M_\odot+ m_{comet})}{2a} \]

Here, \(G(M_\odot+ m_{comet}) \approx GM_\odot = \mu_1 = \mu\), so we calculate the velocity

\[ v^2 = (\ddot x^2 +\ddot y^2 +\ddot z^2 ) = G(M_\odot+ m_{comet})(\frac{2}{r_1}-\frac{1}{a}) \\ \implies v^2 = \mu(\frac{2}{r}-\frac{1}{a}) \]

$$

$$

For the angular momentum of the orbit

\[ \vec{h} = \vec{r} \times \vec{\dot r} = n\sqrt{a(1-e^2)GM} \\ = n\sqrt{a(1-e^2)\mu} = constant \] $$

For the orbital energy,

\[ E = \vec h \cdot \vec n \\ &= h \cdot n \cos{i} \\ &= n\sqrt{a(1-e^2)\mu} \cdot n \cos{i} \] the \(z'\) component of \(\vec h\) is given by

\[ 2n(\dot x' y' - x' \dot y') =  h \cdot n \cos{i} \] So, Jocobi integral \(C_J\) becomes

\[ C_J &= 2V - v^2 \\ &= 2(\frac{\mu_1}{r_1}+\frac{\mu_2}{r_2}) + 2n(\dot x' y' - x' \dot y') - ( \dot x'^2 + \dot y'^2 + \dot z'^2 ) \\ & (\because \frac{\mu_1}{r_1} \gg \frac{\mu_2}{r_2}\approx 0 , \mu_1\approx\mu, r_1 \approx r ) \\ &= \frac{2\mu}{r} + 2n(\dot x' y' - x' \dot y')- v^2 \\ & = \frac{2\mu}{r} + 2h \cdot n \cos{i} - (\frac{2\mu}{r}- \frac{\mu}{a}) \\ &= \frac{\mu}{a} + 2h \cdot n \cos{i} \\ & = \frac{\mu}{a} + 2 \cdot n\sqrt{a(1-e^2)\mu} \cdot n \cos{i} ~~~~ (\because n= 1) \\ &= \frac{\mu}{a} + 2\sqrt{a(1-e^2)\mu} \cdot \cos{i} \]

\[ C_J = \frac{\mu}{a} + 2\sqrt{a(1-e^2)\mu} \cdot \cos{i}  \approx constant \]

If the comet is not close to Jupiter, so that \(\frac{1}{r_2}\) is always small to neglect the \(\mu_2\) term in Equation , then we have

\[ T_J = \frac{1}{2} C_J = \frac{\mu}{2a} + \sqrt{a(1-e^2)\mu} \cdot \cos{i}  \approx constant \]

Therefore, the approximate relationship with the orbital element of the comet before and after encountering with Jupiter is given by

\[ T_J = \frac{\mu}{2a} + \sqrt{a(1-e^2)\mu} \cdot \cos{i} = \frac{\mu}{2a'} + \sqrt{a'(1-e'^2)\mu} \cdot \cos{i'} \label{10} \] By measuring the orbital elements of the comet, we can check whether the comet is new observed, or is the passage of a previously observed comet scattered by a close encounter with Jupiter. the result is shown as Equation \(\ref{10}\).
